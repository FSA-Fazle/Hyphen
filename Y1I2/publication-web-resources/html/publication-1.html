The Government of Ontario has categorized ‘South Asians’ as a single cultural group by proclaim-ing the month of May as the ‘South Asian Heritage Month’ (Ghosh, 2013). Government instrumentalities, along with media and academic practices, have come together to define ‘South Asians’ as a ‘race’ in Canada, which has serious political implications, as they inﬂuence the perceptions and attitudes of Canadians. No academic discipline or spatiality has escaped this process of ‘othering’, and a rewrit-ing of the terms of citizenship and democracy in society at large is need-ed to overcome racism (Kobayashi & Peak, 2002).

The cultural construction of the term ‘desi’ within dominant discourse empowers a specific group, excludes others, and mediates a flattened con-ception of the term that constitutes a form of institutionalized racism (Ghosh, 2013). Juxtaposing the lived experience of young Canadians from the diaspora communities against those discursive acts confirms the presence of that dominant narrative and representation. The focus group discussion held on October 4, 2025, in Brantford has further broadened our understanding of the concept of ‘desi.’ These young minds revealed the un-derlying tension within the groups as well as the ideologies hidden in plain sight, which demand a re-imagining of the term and could be viewed as an act of resistance (Hall, 1996; Foucault, 1978).

Introduction

The term ‘Desi’ denotes the marginal space that refers to a ‘South Asian’ identity; yet, in reality, it encompasses a concept that flattens all the nuances within the category and the geopolit-ical space it refers to. Although it is generally accepted that Bangladesh, India, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka are in-tegral parts of the geographical region identified as ‘South Asia’, Afghanistan, Bhutan, Myanmar, or Nepal are often included or excluded arbitrarily.

There is no monolithic South Asian culture, but at a certain level, there are shared customs, values, and/or cultur-al traditions that bring these groups under a unified umbrella. Howev-er, a predominantly Hindi/Punjabi cultural manifestation in any ‘South Asian’ representation is problematic. They do not adequately represent the cultural diversity of the region, which is a project “to map, categorize and denote ex-colonial non-white bodies” (Ghosh, 2013, p. 39). The govern-ment’s use of census categories and data collection methods not only con-ceals any political agenda of dominant groups but also frequently serves to ‘othering’ the marginal populations (Appadurai, 1993; Kobayashi, 1993; Anthias, 1999). In the ‘South Asian’ case, Ghosh (2013) argued, “...is not just how the data are collected, but also why and how these are aggregat-ed and further categorized that reﬂect ethnocentricism and a Eurocentric knowledge production process” (p. 40).

Discussion and Analysis

Arham Hasan moved to Brantford from Bangladesh at the age of two or three. He attended Central Public School as his first elementary school, where most of the kids were White. He had never heard the label ‘desi’ until he moved to Mississauga, a way more diverse place with many differ-ent communities.

I would say it means somewhere from around the Indian subcon-tinent. So, like India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, like just that general vicinity of various cultures that hold certain values and truths to themselves…But when I say ‘desi’, when I'm talking about my desi friends, that's usu-ally the group that I'm speaking about. (Arham, 2025)

Zarlish Qazi, from the Pakistani diaspora, was born in Sharjah, UAE but has lived here most of her life and considers Canada her home. For her, ‘desi’ has always meant Pakistan and Pakistani and Indian people, because when she was young, she saw the ma-jority of people that made up the area where she lived. There weren’t many Bengali or Afghani, so she didn't even think they could include them either.

I think it's also a word that I would use when I'm talking to my non-brown friends…‘desi’ was like a common term I always heard…And I didn't know that it also had a negative connotation to some people…When I think of that, I just think of anyone who's brown, anyone from South Asia, and what-not. (Qazi, 2025)

Arham claimed it’s usually the Indi-an voice that speaks out the loudest amongst the rest within the ‘desi’ umbrella, but believed it is due to their sheer numbers. The vast major-ity of the ‘desi’ people in his school in Mississuaga, maybe 70 or 80 of them, were all from India. There was even discussion whether India itself was a suitable label for where someone's from, because there are so many cul-tures within India as well.

Raiena Haque was born in Bangla-desh and grew up there for seven years before migrating to Canada. For her, the term ‘desi’ can be perceived as either positive or negative depending on the context. She argued that a lot of the smaller voices are generalized to the point where people only have this one main image when they think of ‘desi’. Haque noted that the term is meant for people outside of her com-munity, as she can claim her Bengali ethnicity when she is within her own community. Thus, to her, “It can be positive in the sense that it unifies that community” (Haque, 2025).

However, it can be negative because it generalizes all these different subcul-tures and religions into a single group. She explained how people use deroga-tory labels, such as “curry muncher” or similar terms, to refer to the ‘South Asian’ community. So it's obviously in a negative connotation, but that's where they find comfort when they talk to their friends, like “Oh, we're all ‘desi,’ we're in this together.”
