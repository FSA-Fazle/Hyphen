Because Bolly-wood is part of the ‘desi’ identity, but it's not like ‘his people’ who are mak-ing it. So, it troubled him and became an internal sort of crisis.

At that mo-ment of such a sacred type of ceremo-ny, where two people are being wed, they’re both Bengali and nearly all the guests are Bengalis. Yet they're playing 99% Bollywood songs. Chowdhury concluded that it seems more like a culture or nationality being represented rather than the language. However, Haque argued that often the assumption is that just because someone looks ‘desi’, they can speak Hindi. But culture is also a big part of it, and often it relates to Hindu culture. So she concluded that it's a mix of nationality and language, and it’s hard to choose between the two.

Shakil Hasan added that Raiena Haque had answered Chowdhury’s question when she mentioned about Bengalis playing Hindi songs at marriage ceremonies: “So, if it were for language, then we would not have

played those. So, it is really a cultural thing. We have adopted the culture of Hindi, India, or something like that” (Hasan, 2025).

On the other hand, Monowar argued that identifying as ‘desi’ depends on the process of assimilation or coloni-zation. He argued that when people are assimilated into a culture without being colonized or forced into, they assimilate on their own terms. How-ever, when identifying with ‘desi’, as a group, due to a lot of generalizing, someone can't choose how they assimilate. For example, people talk about Indian drivers and their crazy driving culture, which is not a piece of culture he would want to carry forward. In that sense, there are a lot of negative connotations, espe-cially when it comes to assimilating (Monowar, 2025).

Carmen Sandhu, an Indian, Punjabi, and Sikh, thinks the term ‘desi’ is mis-leading. Sandhu said that not every-one perceives this word the same way, as it is very subjective:

To group all South Asian cultures into one term is very difficult, which is why the term can be con-voluted in itself. In Western cul-tures, they're not very well versed in the diversity of those cultures, so referring to desi can be a huge generalization, grouping it into one culture, which diminishes the diversity and differences between cultures that should be celebrated. Hence, making them one shared culture isn't an accurate depiction, as it can also contribute to stereo-typing and misunderstanding of the cultures of different commu-nities. (Sandhu, as cited by Haque, 2025)

Sajjad Hossain pointed out that peo-ple don't have a choice, as they would be tagged even if they denounce their past. He provided an example of a group of American kids who won the math Olympiads and appeared to be of East Asian descent. So, people were joking: Are they really American, or are they Chinese?

Arham brought the contrast between American identity, which is seen as a melting pot of cultures, and Cana-dian identity, which is viewed more as a mosaic with each culture like a specific tile. In the Canadian context, Arham argued, each of the cultures is different, unique, and distinct, but collectively, they make one big Cana-dian image. So, he argued, there’s a huge difference between being ‘desi’ in Canada versus being ‘desi’ in America.

Haque mentioned that one of her friends Hayyan Hadi told her that the word ‘desi’ makes him think of “the stereotypical Brown household with the smell of chatta masala and loud conversations!” Hayyan preferred to call himself Brown. But then you think of ‘South Asians' when you think of Brown, so all these little things come together, and it's really convoluted, as there are lots of nuances to it.
