Bangladeshi-Canadian Health Care Workers in Ontario: Hope, Challenge, and Opportunity

Dr. Faisal Shams

Ontario’s health care system is going through one of its most demanding periods in recent memo-ry. Hospitals, long-term care homes, and community clinics are all feeling the pressure of staffing shortages. Yet, for many internationally trained professionals — including those from Bangladesh — entering the workforce in their trained field is still a long and often emotionally challenging jour-ney.

According to the Financial Account-ability Office of Ontario (2025), the job vacancy rate in the province’s health care and social assistance sec-tor reached 5.0% in 2024, reflecting the ongoing struggle to meet work-force needs. The demand for skilled workers is undeniable. Still, newcom-ers often discover that high demand does not automatically translate into easy entry.

For many Bangladeshi-trained doc-tors, nurses, and therapists, the hur-dles are familiar:

Licensing and credential assess-ment through regulated colleges.

Local Canadian experience, which is often required before full regis-tration.

Language proficiency and clinical judgment, essential for safe and effective patient care in Ontario’s environment.

These steps can take months or even years. Behind each journey is a story of sacrifice — often parents working survival jobs, young graduates prepar-ing for exams late at night, or families navigating the uncertainty of starting over in a new country.

Despite the challenges, there is real progress and meaningful support

emerging. One of the most encour-aging developments is the Supervised Practice Experience Partnership (SPEP), a collaboration between the College of Nurses of Ontario (CNO) and Ontario Health. Through SPEP, internationally educated nurses can complete 140+ supervised hours to meet evidence-of-practice require-ments. As of early 2024, more than 3,000 nurses had completed the pro-gram, and the Ontario government has now made it permanent, ensuring that more internationally trained nurses can transition into the work-force with confidence.

For physicians, the pathway remains longer, but there are signs of improve-ment. According to the Canadian Post-M.D. Education Registry (CA-PER), 1,448 internationally trained physicians were in residency training in Ontario in 2024 (2025). A signifi-cant number come from South Asian countries, including Bangladesh, showing that more IMGs are gradual-ly entering the clinical system.

At the community level, the Bangla-deshi population in Canada contin-ues to grow — now numbering over 75,000 nationwide, with most living in Ontario. For new health profes-sionals, this community has become an essential source of mentorship and motivation. Whether it’s sharing study materials, offering guidance through the licensing process, or simply providing moral support, the Bangla-deshi-Canadian community plays a vital role in helping newcomers find their footing.

The journey for Bangladeshi-Canadi-an health care workers is not simple. It demands patience, financial sac-rifice, and resilience. But thousands are succeeding — securing positions in hospitals, clinics, and long-term care facilities across Ontario. They are contributing not only as skilled professionals but as compassionate individuals who bring a strong work

ethic, cultural empathy, and a deep commitment to patient care.

Their stories reflect determination, hope, and the belief that with the right support, dreams built in Bangla-desh can truly flourish in Canada. n

Dr. Harun Ur Rashid

Faces of Racism

It was probably 1976, I was working in a British University on a project finding out the optimum water con-tent in a specific type of resin, which was being used in equipment in sub-marines, for my PhD. One day, as a part of the program, all postgraduate research students in our department went to visit a local coal mine. The supervisor of the coal mine, who was responsible for giving us the tour, gave us safety glasses and the head lamps. While giving us the instructions about what to do and what not to do, while going down in an elevator and walk-ing around in the mine following him, he looked at me and said, “Keep on smiling so that we know where you are.” I was the only coloured person in the group. Everybody laughed. I laughed too. The idea was that in the dark, he would not see me but he would notice my white teeth. He would see the rest because of their white skin. I trust you can not make this kind of race biased comment in present Britain. I must point out, I did not face any explicit racism within the campus. I was invited to lunch in my supervisor’s house. I played tennis in the Staff Tennis Club. Outside in gen-eral, racism was rampant. Politicians like Enoch Powell, openly advocated the removal of all black people and “Pakis” (South Asians were called Pakis, as the predominant group that came from East and West Pakistan). National Front, a right wing pressure group, used to march through the immigrant neighbourhood yelling, “Pakis go home!”

Fast forward to 1989. I was working as a sales person for Sears making house calls selling siding. On one visit, I completed my sales pitch to an elderly couple in Orangeville, north west of Toronto. The lady looked at me and said with a straight face, “For people like you, we had to leave Toronto and move here.” I could not make a sale.

We bought a house at the north end of Brantford in 1995 and moved there

from Hamilton. I used to commute to Brantford to my school every day tak-ing HWY 2. HWY 403 was not com-pleted at that time. I still remember the look we got from the staff when we went to the local grocery store, probably Zehrs on Fairview Road. The look was as if they saw an alien family come to shop! We realized later that not many coloured people were living in Brantford at that time. There was no Bangladeshi family. We heard that there was a West Bengal family. Unfortunately, we never met them. A Bangladeshi family came to live and work in Brantford after a few years.

We moved to our present home in 2006. No “Hi”, “Hello” from any of our neighbours. After a few months, when I was working on the front yard, the neighbour, an elderly lady from the opposite house, approached me and said, “We are very happy to see you guys looking after your property.” As if the expectation was the opposite.

At our work place, a local high school, both myself and my wife did not ex-perience any obvious racist behaviour from our colleagues. Some students, including some brown kids, occasion-ally started talking in an Indian accent when passing us in the hall. Brown kids mimicking Indian accents! Rac-ism is living well and good in every race.

Our son, who was born in Glasgow, U.K., was in elementary school in the early nineties. He had a different experience. Name calling, “Paki”, and bullying were regular incidents. In Hamilton, three white kids used to push him to the wall and run after him at the end of the school day. He did not complain to the teachers and also did not tell us, as he feared things would be worse if he did. Things were better in high school and in univer-sity. At his work place as a branch manager of a distributing and man-ufacturing company, he overheard comments often like, “these Muslims and their bombs,” after 9/11. While he was in Alberta (2010 - 2016), busi-ness associates told him that some businesses would not deal with him because of his skin colour.

Our daughter started her elemen-

tary schooling in Brantford in the beginning of 2000. Her experience with racism was limited to staring, not much communication with her. Occasionally, the adults would ask her, “Where are you from? Your English is good”. Once she mentioned she was born in Hamilton, the next question was, “ Where are your parents from?” Her most disturbing experience came from her high school English teacher, the same school where her father worked and mother still works, possibly in 2009/2010. The teacher was talking about different religious practices. “The Christians do this. The Jews do this. And the Muslims bomb people.” And I thought my colleagues were close to angels! The teacher later apologized after some students pro-tested. My daughter and her husband bought a house in Sturgeon Falls, near North Bay, in June this year. Her one neighbour, a Quebecer, once told her that she did not see many people there with her skin tone and immediately asserted that she was not against the people with brown colour.

From 1988 to 2025, lots of water has passed through Ontario lake. Lots of coloured families have settled down in different Canadian cities. In 1989, in Brampton, (currently it is known as Brown Town,) my son was the only brown kid. Now in his daughter's class in Brantford, almost two thirds of her classmates are coloured.
