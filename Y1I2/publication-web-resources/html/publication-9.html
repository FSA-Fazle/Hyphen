Coming into a new country and adjusting to its culture is hard, especially for kids and young adults. Most kids are drawn to the lyrics of Rap music and will listen to artists like Drake and Jay Z. So, in this issue, we will put the spotlight on an artist who is bridging the gap between Rap and Desi music (specifically Bangla music).

Anik Khan1 was born in Dhaka, Bangladesh January 13th, 1989. His father was a freedom fighter in the Bangladesh Liberation War and was also involved in politics. In 1993 when Anik was only 4 his family migrated to the United States.2

Anik was drawn to music from an early age. He would create “beats” (com-pose hip-hop or rap music) on FL Studio (musical software). Like many first generation of young adults Anik went through an identity crisis, where he questioned who he was and what his culture was.

After high school, Anik went to Full Sail University in Florida to study recording arts engineering. He would often fly back to New York, where his family had settled in 2011, in order to record his music. He had put all his efforts into networking and trying to start his musical career. He would sleep on couches and would meet Fadia Kander who managed him for a few years. Anik had finally released his first single “I Don’t Know Yet in July 2015”.3

This was just the beginning of his success as well his struggles. In 2017, he released the album “Kites” and started to see success. With the success of his album he was offered many tours in London, Amsterdam, Porto, Berlin. However, he had to pass on all those offers since he was not yet an Ameri-can citizen - despite living in America since he was 4. It was not until 2021 when Anik had finally experienced success. In that year he received his citizenship and released 2 EPs (Extended Play), “Denied” and “Approved”.4

Anik represents the hardship that most immigrant families face. His story is inspirational and shows how you can find success after many years of hardship.

Cultural Shock of a Bangladeshi immigrant in

Canadian Corporate culture!

It is nothing new as we all immi-grants face this kind of situation to some extent. Like a simpleton, I thought, I would be able to design my own set of desires as it is a free country. But NO! I could not. When it comes to cultural shock, religious shocks are also hitched on as a side dish. Two situations that still strike my mind:

Situation 1: It happened in “ABC” Company (Cana-dian owned), where I used to work. There, I was the only brown, small, hijabi woman, and definitely it was bugging me, when I noticed that my colleagues had a hard time hiding their discomfort and acting normal around me. All of them were well informed, specifically about my particular choice for Halal meat or vegetarian option. But, in an office party, they forgot to order that certain kind of food, and asked me to eat the bread in a sandwich by removing the salami inside it. I cordially declined, and I felt like a black sheep of that certain company.

Situation 2: On the flip side, the “XYZ” compa-ny, a well recognized Canadian company showered me with love and respect to every aspect of my culture and religion. During one of our office potlucks, I could not at-tend because I was fasting for Ramadan. My boss noticed that, and after Eid-ul-Fitr, she organized a small party especially for me and other Muslim colleagues, where foods were arranged keeping in mind about our choice, which showed her willingness to respect our religion, and culture. I felt so honored, recognized, loved, and honestly, this was beyond my imagination that they would respect my belief to this extent.

At the end, I would say, many of my close people may ask “if you would like to continue your own belief, religion, culture according to your comfort then you should not have come to Canada, why have you come af-ter all?”. I prefer not to answer this question. My belief is: why everyone's dream needs to be the same? Why are we being so judgemental of our own people? It is my belief that my purpose is to stay on my faith, which is to satisfy my own Creator, and this is my purpose to come into this world. Have I ever asked to those judgy people, “what is their purpose of coming to this world?”

Implement International Mother Language Day City Celebra-tions and Dedicate a Monument

The Issue:

Petition to officially celebrate and commision a monument commemorating the International Mother Language Day in the City of Brantford

As a Bangladeshi Diaspora, we deeply value the diversity and multiculturalism that our city embodies. It is what makes our community vibrant, diverse, and wholly unique. We propose to celebrate our diversity even more visibly by officially com-memorating the International Mother Language Day. In an effort to promote linguistic and cultural diversity and multilingualism across the globe, UNESCO declared the 21st of February as the International Mother Language Day in 1999. This year marks the 25th anniversary of that proclama-tion. Furthering this legacy, we propose that our city officially recognize and celebrate this day, cherishing the numerous languages that contribute to our multicultural identity.In addition, to further signify our commitment to celebrating our city's linguistic and cultural diversity, we propose that the city designate a small plot in a local park or anywhere deemed appropriate (no larger than 10'x10') for the monument. This monument would serve as a symbolic representation toward the significance of mother languages and a tribute to our mul-ticultural roots.Through these actions, we can further solidify the principles of respect, understanding, and diversity that underpin our community. Let’s make our city the beacon of multicultural-ism and linguistic diversity by acknowledging and celebrating International Mother Language Day. Please sign this petition to support these initiatives.
